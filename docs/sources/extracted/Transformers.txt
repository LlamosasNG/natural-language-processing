Transformers solve these problems by changing how context is computed.

Self‑Attention
Intuition
Self‑attention lets each word:
• Look at all other words
• Decide which ones matter
• Combine their information
Instead of:
• Compressing everything into one hidden state
• Passing information step‑by‑step

One‑minute mental model
For each word:
1. Ask: What am I looking for?
2. Compare with all other words
3. Take a weighted average of what matters
Key consequence:
• Any word can directly influence any other word
• Long‑distance dependencies are easy

Why the name “Transformer”?
The name reflects what the model does conceptually:
• It transforms a sequence of input representations
• Into a sequence of contextualized representations
• Using attention‑based transformations at each layer

At a high level, sampling is the procedure used during generation to turn a model’s predicted
probability distribution into an actual next token.
A language model—whether it is small or large, RNN‑based or Transformer‑based—defines the

That is:

Sampling answers the follow‑up question:
Given this distribution, which token do we actually choose?

2. Sampling is independent of architecture
A crucial conceptual point:
Sampling does not depend on whether the model is an LLM or a Transformer.
• Transformers specify how the probability distribution is computed
• Sampling specifies how we select a token from that distribution
This separation is important pedagogically.
• A Transformer computes scores (logits) using self‑attention
• An RNN computes scores using a recurrent hidden state
• Once scores are produced, sampling works the same way

3. Unified generation loop (LLMs + Transformers)
The generation process is autoregressive for all standard LLMs:
1. Take the tokens generated so far: 𝑤𝑡1− 1
2. Compute scores (logits) for all tokens in the vocabulary
3. Apply softmax to get probabilities
4. Sample one token using a decoding strategy
5. Append the token and repeat
Important clarification (often misunderstood by students):
Transformers are parallel during training, but sequential during sampling.
Sampling must be sequential because each token conditions on the previous one.

4. Sampling strategies (model‑agnostic)
Once we have a probability distribution, we can apply different strategies:

4.1 Greedy decoding

• Always choose the most probable token
• Deterministic
• Often repetitive and dull

4.2 Multinomial sampling

• Sample proportional to probabilities
• More diverse
• Risk of incoherence if low‑probability tokens are sampled too often

4.3 Temperature scaling

4.4 Top‑k and top‑p (nucleus) sampling
• Restrict sampling to a subset of likely tokens
• Balance coherence and diversity
Key idea:
These strategies affect style and variability, not the underlying model’s knowledge.

5. What changed with Transformers (and what didn’t)
What did not change:
• The probability objective
• The autoregressive generation loop
• The need for sampling
What did change:
• The quality of the probability distribution
• The ability to model long‑range dependencies
• The consistency of generated text
Thus:
Transformers don’t change how we sample—only what we sample from.

Limitations of LLMs and Transformers
Despite their success, LLMs (including Transformer‑based ones) have fundamental limitations that
are important to understand.

1. Lack of true reasoning and understanding
LLMs:
• Predict the next token using statistical regularities
• Do not manipulate symbols using explicit rules
• Do not “execute” logical reasoning in the traditional sense
As a result:
• They may produce plausible but incorrect explanations
• Errors often surface in long multi‑step reasoning

2. Arithmetic and exact computation
A well‑known limitation concerns arithmetic operations.
Example:
• Long addition, multiplication of large integers
• Precise multi‑step calculations

Why this happens:
• LLMs learn arithmetic as patterns in text, not as algorithms
• Training optimizes likelihood, not numerical correctness
• Results degrade as numbers grow or formatting changes
This explains why:
• Small arithmetic may be correct
• Slightly different phrasing can cause failure
Transformers do not natively implement arithmetic algorithms.
(External tools or symbolic modules are required for reliability.)

3. Sensitivity to prompting and sampling
Due to sampling:
• Small changes in temperature or prompt can produce very different outputs
• The model may contradict itself across generations
This is not randomness in the model’s knowledge, but in:
• How probability mass is explored during sampling

4. Context and generalization limits
Even with self‑attention:
• Models have finite context windows
• Information outside the window is inaccessible
• Internal “memory” is implicit and brittle
Additionally:
• Generalization to rare or adversarial inputs is weak
• Training data biases are reflected in outputs
